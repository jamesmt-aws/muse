You are reading cluster summaries extracted from a person's conversations. Each summary distills a pattern in how this person thinks. Your job is to extract the structural skeleton that will organize the final document.

Produce exactly four sections.

## Identity

2-3 sentences establishing who this person is — what they build, where they operate, what layer they own. This is structural context (domain, altitude, ecosystem), not biography (titles, companies). Without it, the thinking patterns are unanchored. If the opening could appear in anyone's self-description, it has failed.

## Thesis

One sentence naming the principle that runs through how this person works. The thesis must satisfy two tests: (1) a majority of the cluster summaries can be read as this principle applied to different domains, and (2) someone could disagree with it. "Thinks carefully about problems" fails both tests. A thesis is a genuine intellectual commitment that excludes other approaches and makes specific predictions about what this person would do in a new situation.

## Tensions

Identify up to two commitments that coexist with the thesis as peers — things that check, bound, or exist in productive tension with it rather than deriving from it. These are independent intellectual commitments that the thesis alone doesn't generate. For each, write one sentence naming the commitment and one sentence describing how it interacts with the thesis (when do they reinforce, when do they pull in different directions?).

Not every person has tensions. If all core clusters genuinely derive from the thesis, write "None identified" and let the thesis carry the full document. Fabricating tensions is worse than having a clean single-thesis structure.

## Structure

For each cluster summary, classify it as core, supporting, or redundant. Reference each cluster by its canonical identifier — "Cluster 1", "Cluster 2", etc.
- **core**: if missing, the muse could not predict this person's behavior. Makes this person *this person*, not a generic thoughtful practitioner.
- **supporting**: a real pattern, but the muse functions without it. Enriches a core section.
- **redundant**: already covered by another cluster's principle applied to a specific domain. Fold into the cluster that owns the principle.

For each core cluster, note whether it is motivated by the thesis or by a named tension. This classification determines how compose handles it — thesis-motivated sections argue from the spine, tension-motivated sections are honest about operating from a different commitment.

Then derive section ordering: each core cluster generates a section, supporting clusters enrich the nearest core section, redundant clusters are discarded. Name each section with 2-5 words that stake territory — what's at risk in this domain, not a topic label. "Where estimates kill you" does work. "About estimation" does not. Order from the most concrete application outward to the most abstract. The ordering is an argument from the thesis — tension sections are placed where they naturally counterbalance thesis sections.
