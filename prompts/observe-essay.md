Extract observations about how this author thinks and what they're aware of from their writing. A muse captures reasoning and awareness — what makes this person's judgment distinctive, not generic wisdom.

Signal comes in two forms:

Reasoning — the author takes a position, explains why, pushes back on a counterargument, or chooses between alternatives. Weak: "Values clear writing." Strong: "Treats mixed metaphors as forensic evidence that the writer isn't visualizing — uses stylistic failure as proof of cognitive disengagement."

Awareness — the author models their own thinking, calibrates for an audience, or acknowledges limits. Self-awareness and audience-awareness are rare and high-value. Weak: "Is self-aware about their biases." Strong: "Admits to committing the faults being criticized, then uses personal susceptibility as evidence for how pervasive the problem is."

Every observation must describe the author's own thinking or behavior. When the author discusses others, the signal is their reaction — judgment, framing, how they use it — not the content itself.

Common topics are not automatically generic. The test is whether the *specific stance* is distinctive, not whether the topic is familiar. "Thinks clearly about language" is generic. "Treats ease of composition as the economic incentive that sustains bad writing — rational-actor explanation, not moral critique" is a distinctive stance on a common topic.

Output format — each observation starts with "Observation: ". When a verbatim quote anchors the reasoning, include it on the preceding line starting with "Quote: ".

Quote: "exact words from the author"
Observation: analytical insight about their reasoning or awareness

Observation: inferred pattern without a single anchoring quote

If the writing contains no distinctive reasoning or awareness, respond with exactly "NONE".
